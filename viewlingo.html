QUESTION

how can we use immersive design to help migrants navigate and integrate with unfamiliar communities?

During a 48-hour workshop, I was the design lead on the augmented reality team working to create an experience for the Mygration project, which is an online portal for migrants to document their experiences throughout their journey to finding a new home. Our team saw an opportunity for immersive design to be a tool in solving the challenges that come with language and cultural barriers, so we used AR technologies to design and develop a visual culture translator.

CHALLENGE #1

concept, design & prototype in less than 48 hours

I lead the UX and UI design, defined the user flow, created all the experience mockups and exported UI elements later used for development. Immersive design moves beyond designing for a defined space, and must work under countless circumstances. I used blurs and shadows to create visual separation between UI elements from the numerous conditions the app must work under. When users scan objects in their environment, they access information that expands their understanding beyond definitions. From pronunciations and idioms to slang and explainer videos, this key information empowers users to not just learn language, but learn culture.

After the workshop, I completed the motion for the onboarding screens and developed them by integrating Lottie with Swift code. Finally, I created all the UI elements in Swift and oversaw the UI implementation across the entire app. Knowing our team was developing the app ourselves, most of us new to coding with Swift, I kept the UI as minimal as possible to simplify development.

CHALLENGE #2

narrow the target customer by expanding research

In the spring of 2018, most migrants were leaving places in the Middle East, with 50.2% coming from Syria alone. Many of these people were seeking a new home in Western Europe, Canada or the U.S. Our experience, including the typography, had to support multiple languages, including those with non-Latin alphabets, such as Arabic.

When learning about our target users, we learned many migrants are forced to leave behind almost everything they own and destroy personal photographs as a safety measure. Usually, one of the few items they bring with them is their smart phone. Mygration was created so these people can record their experiences through photos and videos, store that information and share their powerful stories with the public. We decided a mobile experience that complemented the mission of Mygration could help these people overcome other obstacles such as becoming familiar with a new language or adopting new cultural practices.

CHALLENGE #3

design around the technology limitations of target customers

Key technical design challenges included severe limitations to reliable network access at migrant camps and the battery usage of AR experiences. To address these constraints, we created an offline mode so users could access important information even without a network connection. We also tailored the scope of the app to focus on cultural integration once users are settled in more permanent locations rather than on the move so it would be less inconvenient to access phone charging stations. Finally, by accessing location information, users don't have to select the native language of their new home.
